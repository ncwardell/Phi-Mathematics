# Meeting Points as Constructive Interference

> **Theorem 33 (Meeting Points -- Constructive Resonance):** *Meeting points are the only depths at which stable particle structures can crystallize, because stability requires reinforcement from multiple independent self-referential modes.*

## Definition

**Meeting Point:** A **meeting point** is a positive integer that appears in the recurrence sequences (Fibonacci-type or Lucas-type) of two or more distinct metallic families. It is a value where two different winding modes of the self-referential spiral produce the same integer -- constructive interference between the fundamental and an overtone, or between two overtones.

## Statement

Meeting points are the only depths at which stable particle structures can crystallize, because stability requires reinforcement from multiple independent self-referential modes.

## Proof

By Theorem 1.3, stability requires witnessing that cannot be reduced to a single self-cancelling pair. A structure sustained by a single metallic sequence exists at only one "frequency" -- a single self-referential mode. It has no independent confirmation of its own existence.

A meeting point, by contrast, is an integer where two or more independent self-referential processes agree. The structure at that depth is witnessed from multiple independent modes simultaneously. This is the higher-order analog of the triangle: just as three nodes are the minimum for stable witnessing (Theorem 1.3), multiple metallic resonances are the minimum for stable particle formation.

A single-mode structure is to a meeting-point structure as a two-node pair is to a triangle: the former has no independent confirmation and is subject to collapse; the latter is self-reinforcing from multiple directions. $\blacksquare$
